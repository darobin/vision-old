This document captures W3C's vision for action in the world.

It was created based on a brainstorming breakout during Team Day 2013. It is an evolving, unofficial, not-particularly-sanctioned, likely-wrong-in-many-places write-up of our collective thinking.

The Web Society

WARNING: at this point, all of the content has been written by Robin (hi!) and I have made no effort whatsoever to tame the natural tendencies of my writing. The final product will sound less crazy.

An organisation that does not envision and continuously strive to effect a state of the world sufficiently aligned with its goals that it will then, having attained its endgame, disband and cease its activities of its own accord runs the risk of progressively renouncing its ambitions in order solely to perpetuate its own existence.

Such goals may be lofty. They may be far away and hard to reach. The path leading to them from the present situation may be murky and strenuous, if it can be seen at all, but the destination must be clearly conceived for the organisation to continue applying itself towards their realisation and escape the drift towards a self-preservation of the means over the ends and its attendant bureaucracy.

These goals cannot be solely technical. Technology devoid of social embodiment is at best a toy; social aspirations without the technical means to deploy themselves are but fantasy. Technology does not create its social counterpart by fiat; and vice versa. A harmonious society demands that humankind's tools and laws be crafted in unison. It is thus the fundamental objective of the World Wide Web Consortium to steward an unfinished revolutionary technology in an incompletely reformed social order until this revolution is completed and in accord with its social correlates.

The principles laid out henceforth are those we believe must be those of the Web and the society in which it unfolds, but also those that our community must exemplify in its own behaviour on the way there. They are therefore meant to inform all aspects of our work be they technical or organisational, internal to our functioning or exposed externally through our contributions to the broader fora of society

The Web finds its roots in science, and has grown through a predilection for rational discourse, non-coercive, open to participation, and discursive. It would be incongruous to believe that the beneficent promise of the Web can be realised through the political and ethical technology of the pre-scientific ages.

Systems which foster concentrated control are from the point of view of technological progress reactionary. Web technology must therefore maximise decentralisation, as must the process through which it is produce since any alliance of people, no matter how well-intentioned, will see its abilities residing a long way this side of omniscience and omnipotence.

At the same time, it is necessary to establish for voluntary associations of the Web's constituencies rights which they can enforce agains magnates and majorities, monopolies and mobs. Stewardship of the Web must therefore proceed through a venue within which control is relinquished as much as it can be, but which can equally foster the cooperation of many. Such a framework can prosper because good faith collaboration has ceased to be an other-worldly aspiration and become the working doctrine of practical people.

The Web is universal in its reach and conviction, it is for everyone in a world in which all humans are persons and the human person is inviolable.

It is therefore a goal integral to this organisation that the Web be not just available to everyone, but further that it work for everyone irrespective of the individual characteristics that may set people apart, that everyone can freely publish to it, both safely and with expectations of privacy commensurate with the information which they release to the public.

As such, the Web adheres to and advocates the fundamental tenets of democracy, defined not through the logistics of voting — which by themselves offer insufficient guarantees for the inviolability of all — but through basic human rights, independent courts to enforce them, and free media to monitor them. It is a democracy of proactive inclusiveness, of fierce independence, and of ruthless dedication to fair adjudication, including against oneself.

In 1937, Walter Lippmann wrote that “there are important differences between lions and tigers, even between African and Indian lions. But from the point of view of, let us say, a goat or a lamb, the common characteristics of all the great carnivores are more significant than their differences.” From the point of view of a citizen, the common characteristics of those who pry on their safety and privacy are more significant than the differences in their purported political aims or business models.

It would be impossible for the Web to flourish if in doing so it exhausted its resources. It is thus undeniable that conservation, in its broadest sense, including in its coverage the long-term preservation of the documents we produce, the vernacular documentation of our culture, and the natural resources that sustain our technological ecosystem, counts amongst our paramount obligations for there is no preservation of the Web that can simultaneously destroy the patrimony of our children.

In order to reach the Web we want, we must enable agency at a level comparable to the needs of the task at hand. The principles outlined here are intentionally simple, the complexity of policy having to be inversely proportional to the complexity of the affairs that it governs, for while a few things can be directed much, many things can be administered only a little.

The need for decentralised control outlined above makes it possible for new agents not yet established to act decisively within the Web without needing to gain permission or being otherwise blocked by established participants.

Along with the permission granted by decentralisation and openness, interoperability makes it possible for participants to focus on the creative aspects of their work rather than on having to solve lower-level issues time and again in order to deliver worldwide. Note that interoperability is naturally something that we strive for at the technological level, but is also a feature of the legal system. Regulatory frameworks must as much as possible be interoperable at the global level in order to enable the production of services that do not need to be repurposed for specific countries, but rather can nimbly be deployed world wide. Such a worldwide interoperable deployment framework naturally encompasses networks that are neutral with respect to their payloads.

In order to be effective, this interoperability must come at reasonable cost, and enable the creation of powerful, fast programs. It must shield individuals and SMEs from inequality in bargaining power and the costs associated with a polity of pressure groups.

Deployed globally, the enabling agency of interoperability will lead to a Web of abundant resources exchanged in an open market (by which we don't necessarily mean solely monetarily).

These principles are not intended to be cast in stone in perpetuity; rather they demand to be challenged as often and as virulently as needed. It is our goal to account for frictions and disturbances both on the Web and in our actions as a community for those mark the points where there is conflict to be overcome, be it social or technological. We strive through progressive discovery to civilise ourselves, and this process is by no means completed.

Our overarching goal is for humankind to become adult and to replace paternal authority with fraternal association. We simply wish to do our part in committing the destiny of civilisation to the whole genius of humankind, to be a free conspiracy to fulfil the promises of our time.
